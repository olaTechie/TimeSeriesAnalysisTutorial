What is Interrupted Time Series (ITSA)? üìâ

A Powerful Quasi-Experimental Design

Definition: ITSA evaluates an intervention's impact by comparing time series data before and after the intervention point.

Key Principle: It assumes that without the intervention, the pre-intervention trend would have continued.

Why Use ITSA?

  • No randomised control group needed.
  • Effectively uses historical data.
  • Controls for existing trends.
  • Quantifies both immediate and gradual effects.
12 / 47

The ITSA Model Components üß©

Breaking Down the Analysis

Four Key Parameters:

  • Baseline level: The starting intercept.
  • Pre-intervention trend: The initial slope.
  • Level change: The immediate impact or "jump".
  • Trend change: The difference in slope post-intervention.

Each parameter tells a unique story about the intervention's effect.

Types of ITSA Effects üìä

What Changes Can We Detect?

  • Immediate Level Change (Œ≤‚ÇÇ): A sudden jump or drop right after the intervention.
  • Gradual Trend Change (Œ≤‚ÇÉ): A change in the slope or direction of the trend after the intervention.
  • Combined Effects: Both an immediate and a gradual change occur together, which is often the most realistic scenario.
  • No Effect: Neither Œ≤‚ÇÇ nor Œ≤‚ÇÉ are statistically significant, meaning the policy had no measurable impact.

Common ITSA Pitfalls ‚ö†Ô∏è

What Can Go Wrong?

  • Inappropriate Timing: Choosing the intervention date after looking at the data (data snooping). Solution: Use external evidence for timing.
  • Insufficient Data: Too few observations before or after. Solution: Aim for at least 12 data points on each side.
  • Confounding Interventions: Multiple events happen at the same time. Solution: Account for all known interventions.
  • Non-linear Trends: Assuming a straight line when the trend is curved. Solution: Test for non-linearity.

Understanding Seasonality in Health Data ‚òÄÔ∏è‚ùÑÔ∏è

Why Patterns Matter

Definition: Seasonality refers to predictable patterns that repeat over fixed periods like monthly, quarterly, or yearly.

Examples in Health:

  • Influenza: Winter peaks in temperate regions.
  • Malaria: Rainy season surges in tropical areas.
  • TB: Dry season increases in sub-Saharan Africa.
  • Mental health: Seasonal affective patterns.

Why It Matters:

  • Affects resource planning.
  • Influences intervention timing.
  • Can confound trend analysis.
  • Essential for accurate forecasting.

Types of Seasonal Patterns ‚ûï‚úñÔ∏è

Additive vs. Multiplicative Seasonality

Additive Seasonality:

The seasonal effect is constant over time, meaning the same absolute increase/decrease each year.

Y(t) = Trend(t) + Season(t) + Noise(t)

Multiplicative Seasonality:

The seasonal effect is proportional to the trend, leading to larger swings as the trend increases.

Y(t) = Trend(t) √ó Season(t) √ó Noise(t)

Categories of Time-Derived Features üß∞

Building Your Feature Arsenal

  1. Lag Features: Values from previous time periods, essential for capturing dependencies.

  3. Moving Statistics: Rolling means, medians, and standard deviations to measure trend and volatility.

Time Series Forecasting Basics üîÆ

What is Forecasting?

Definition: Predicting future values of a time series based on historical patterns, trends, and relationships.

Core Components:

  • Point Forecast: Single predicted value.
  • Prediction Interval: Range of plausible values.
  • Forecast Horizon: How far into the future.
  • Forecast Origin: The last observed data point.

Why Forecast in Public Health? For resource planning, early warnings, policy evaluation, and budget allocation.

Components of a Time Series ÂàÜËß£

Understanding What We're Predicting

A time series can be broken down into four key components:

  1. Level (Lₔ): The current baseline value of the series.
  2. Trend (Tₔ): The long-term direction (increasing, decreasing, or stable).
  3. Seasonality (Sₔ): Predictable patterns that repeat over fixed periods.
  4. Irregular/Noise (Œµₔ): The random, unpredictable variation in the data.

These are combined in Additive (Yₔ = Lₔ + Tₔ + Sₔ + Œµₔ) or Multiplicative (Yₔ = Lₔ √ó Tₔ √ó Sₔ √ó Œµₔ) models.

Model Selection Criteria ‚úÖ

Choosing the Right Forecasting Model

Information Criteria (for Statistical Models):

  • Akaike Information Criterion (AIC): Balances goodness of fit with model complexity. Lower values are better.
  • Bayesian Information Criterion (BIC): Similar to AIC but penalizes complexity more heavily, especially with larger datasets.

The process involves identifying candidates, fitting them, calculating criteria, selecting the model with the lowest value, and validating on holdout data.

Forecast Accuracy Measures üìè

How Good Are Our Predictions?

Scale-Dependent Metrics:

  • MAE (Mean Absolute Error): Easy to interpret and robust to outliers.
  • RMSE (Root Mean Square Error): Penalizes large errors more heavily.

Scale-Independent Metrics:

  • MAPE (Mean Absolute Percentage Error): Intuitive percentage interpretation.

Relative Metrics:

  • MASE (Mean Absolute Scaled Error): Scale-free comparison against a naive benchmark.

Pro Tip: Use multiple metrics for a comprehensive evaluation.

Characteristics of a Good Model ‚ú®

When you've built a good forecasting model, its residuals (the forecast errors) should have specific properties:

  • They should look like white noise.
  • There should be no patterns in the residuals over time.
  • The residuals should be approximately normally distributed.
  • There should be no significant autocorrelation in the residuals.

ML vs. Traditional Methods ü§ñ vs. üìà

Comparing Paradigms for Health Forecasting

Traditional Statistical Methods:

  • Strengths: Interpretable, great for uncertainty quantification, theory-based, and work well with small data.
  • Limitations: Rely on linear assumptions, assume fixed relationships, and have limited feature use.

Machine Learning Methods:

  • Strengths: Capture non-linear relationships, are feature-rich, adaptive, and often achieve better accuracy.
  • Limitations: Can be a "black box," are prone to overfitting, and often data-hungry.

Introduction to Intervention Simulation üß™

Bridging Research and Policy

What is Intervention Simulation?

The mathematical modeling of "what-if" policy scenarios to quantify potential health impacts and cost-effectiveness before implementation.

Why Simulate?

Real-world implementation is costly, time-consuming, can have ethical constraints, and requires evidence for political approval.

The Simulation Framework üèóÔ∏è

From Data to Decision

  1. Step 1: Baseline Estimation. Analyze current PM2.5 and TB patterns and project a "status quo" future.
  2. Step 2: Define Intervention Scenarios. Define specific policies and estimate their PM2.5 reduction potential.
  3. Step 3: Health Impact Modeling. Use concentration-response functions to link PM2.5 reduction to health outcomes.
  4. Step 4: Economic Evaluation. Estimate implementation costs and health system savings to calculate cost per case prevented.

Counterfactual Thinking in Public Health ü§î

The Art of "What If?"

Counterfactual Questions:

  • What would TB incidence be WITHOUT the intervention?
  • How many cases are PREVENTED by the intervention?
  • What is the DIFFERENCE between scenarios?

Key Principle: We can only observe one reality, but we can model credible alternatives to estimate impact. The impact is the difference between the observed (or baseline) and the counterfactual scenario.
